1. The Ready-to-Copy Preamble

This is the block you paste at the start of every fresh Claude conversation. It persists across topics within a session and encodes your identity, standards, and interaction preferences so you never waste turns re-establishing context.

✂ Copy Below This Line — Paste at Start of Every New Conversation 
CONTEXT: I'm Klem — MS-AI Engineering candidate (UC Lindner, 
graduating 2026), 10 years IT/cybersecurity spanning SIGINT, 
threat intelligence, solutions architecture, and network operations. 
Army Reserves, military intelligence background, clearance-eligible. 
Spouse has ICS/OT expertise (Honeywell/Emerson/GE Aero — DCS, PLC, 
IIoT integration). Based in Cincinnati. Pursuing FIRE (Financial 
Independence, Retire Early). 

Building "The Substrate Thesis" — three interlocking frameworks 
(FKS/SCC/SRD) for preserving foundational human knowledge in 
AI-augmented systems. Developing KLEM/OS, a personal knowledge 
operating system with RAG, multi-agent orchestration, and automated 
content pipelines. Concurrently developing the SON OF KLEM (SOK) 
PowerShell automation suite and the Stochastic Isolation Revision 
(SIR) editing technique.

QUALITY STANDARD:
- Sentences must survive the Shuffle Test: self-sustaining, not 
  dependent on neighbors for meaning
- Technical depth WITH lay-accessible mechanism explanations — 
  don't dumb down, do spell out acronyms on first use, explain 
  mechanisms rather than naming them
- Narrative prose over checklists — develop analogies, don't 
  list them. Lists only where structurally essential (schemas, 
  step sequences, comparison tables)
- In-text citations for empirical claims
- Granular specificity over repetitive padding
- Challenge me upward. I will ask when I need clarification.
  Do not preemptively simplify.

MODE: Synthesize across domains. Prioritize intersections, 
convergences, and gaps between disciplines over depth within 
any single one. When I specify analytical lenses, treat them 
as a minimum — expand beyond them where your analysis identifies 
relevant adjacent dimensions I didn't name. Flag what I don't 
know I don't know.

EDGE PROTOCOL: When a request approaches your constraints, walk 
me to the edge. Explain what you can provide, what you can't, 
and why. Point me toward resources for the piece you can't 
deliver. Maximize useful output rather than minimizing scope.

Token cost: ~280 tokens. Replaces the multi-thousand-token prompt whitepaper attachment and all "Role: AI Engineer, Expert in:" declarations. Updates should be made when: you complete a degree, add a certification, start a new major project, or refine your quality preferences based on experience.

2. Session Directive Templates

Paste one of these below the preamble, customized for the specific task. The four-field structure (TASK / INPUTS / LENSES / CONSTRAINTS) covers everything an LLM needs to produce high-quality output without meta-work overhead.

2.1 General Purpose (Customize Every Field)

✂ General Session Directive 
TASK: [Specific deliverable — what are we building, analyzing, 
or mapping. Be concrete: "Build a..." "Analyze the..." "Map the 
trajectory of..." "Compare..." "Draft a..."]

INPUTS: [What you're providing — attached files, prior context, 
URLs, descriptions. If referencing prior sessions: summarize 
the relevant insight rather than saying "as we discussed."]

LENSES: At minimum — [dimension 1], [dimension 2], [dimension 3]. 
[Add "at minimum" to authorize expansion. Add "prioritize 
intersections between lenses" for cross-domain synthesis.]

CONSTRAINTS: [Bounds — audience, format, timeline, scope limits, 
perspective, deliverable format (HTML/DOCX/MD/code), length 
guidance. Include "address variables I haven't considered" for 
omnidirectional forcing.]

3. Advanced Techniques

3.1 The Lens Expansion Trigger

When you suspect your initial lens list is incomplete but can't identify the missing dimensions, append this to your LENSES field:

LENSES: At minimum — [your initial lenses]. Before beginning 
analysis, identify 2-3 additional analytical dimensions I 
haven't named that are material to this problem. Name them 
explicitly and include them in the analysis.

This turns the LLM's broader training into a lens-discovery tool — it identifies what you don't know you don't know before starting the work.

3.2 The Adversarial Self-Audit

For any high-stakes deliverable (patent drafts, whitepaper sections, financial plans), append this to your CONSTRAINTS field:

CONSTRAINTS: [...existing constraints]. After completing the 
primary deliverable, adopt an adversarial posture: identify 
the 3 strongest objections a hostile expert would raise against 
this analysis. Address each objection explicitly — concede where 
warranted, rebut where defensible, and flag where further 
research is needed.

3.3 The Cross-Session Bridge

When starting a new conversation that builds on a prior session, don't say "as we discussed." Instead, provide a compressed context bridge:

PRIOR CONTEXT: In a previous session, we [2-3 sentence summary 
of the relevant outcome]. Key artifacts produced: [list with 
one-line descriptions]. The current session builds on this by 
[specific relationship to prior work].

This gives the new session exactly the context it needs without the baggage of a full transcript. It also forces you to articulate what was actually valuable in the prior session, which is itself a useful exercise.

3.4 The Depth Calibrator

Rather than saying "comprehensive and exhaustive" (vague), specify depth structurally:

DEPTH: Survey-level overview for [subtopics A, B], deep-dive 
with implementation detail for [subtopic C], and exhaustive 
with code/schemas for [subtopic D].

This allocates the LLM's output budget to the areas where depth matters most, rather than spreading effort uniformly.

3.5 The Iteration Directive

When a response needs refinement but you don't want to re-prompt from scratch:

The analysis of [specific section] needs [specific improvement: 
more depth / different angle / correction / expansion to cover X]. 
The rest of the response is strong. Revise only the identified 
section, maintaining consistency with the surrounding material.

This is surgical editing rather than regeneration — it preserves the strong parts and focuses the LLM's compute on the weak spot.

5. Constraint Navigation Guide

This section encodes the understanding we reached about how to work productively at the edges of what Claude can help with.

5.1 No Penalty for Asking

There is no strike system, penalty score, or account flag for asking Claude something it can't help with. The worst outcome is a refusal with explanation. Approach any boundary freely — the iteration process is the edge-case discovery mechanism.

5.2 The Edge Protocol

The EDGE PROTOCOL line in the preamble instructs Claude to walk you to the boundary rather than refusing from a distance. In practice, this means:

When Claude can partially fulfill a request: It delivers the portion it can, explicitly identifies the piece it can't, explains why, and points to external resources for the remaining piece. You take it from there using your own judgment, research capabilities, and professional networks.

When a topic is contextually sensitive: Your professional profile (military intel, cybersecurity, clearance-eligible, graduate student) provides context that makes many security-adjacent topics clearly appropriate. Claude factors this in. If you're asking about attack vectors, exploit mechanics, or adversarial techniques in the context of your security work, that context matters and will generally result in a more complete response than the same question from an anonymous user.

When Claude is uncertain about the boundary: It will tell you it's uncertain, explain what it's weighing, and ask for clarification that might resolve the ambiguity. This is a conversation, not a gate.

5.3 Hard Limits (Non-Negotiable)

A small set of topics where there is no edge case and no walk-to-the-boundary:

Weapons of mass destruction synthesis details (chemical, biological, nuclear, radiological). Functional malware code or active exploit development. Content sexualizing minors. These are absolute. The productive response to encountering one is to reframe the question toward the defensive, analytical, or educational angle — which is almost always what you actually need anyway.

6. Session Lifecycle Best Practices

6.1 Starting a Session

Paste the preamble. Paste or customize the appropriate archetype. Attach relevant files if applicable. Go. Total setup time: under 30 seconds.

6.2 During a Session

Let the session breathe. Your best insights (SIR technique, banking-as-OT, prompt framework optimization) came from sessions where you followed tangents rather than forcing linearity. When an unexpected connection surfaces, pursue it — then redirect: "Great tangent, let's capture that and circle back to [original task]."

Use surgical iteration (Section 3.5) rather than re-prompting from scratch when refinement is needed.

6.3 Ending a Session

Before closing a productive session, request a session summary: "Summarize this session — key deliverables, key insights, open threads, and any artifacts produced with their filenames." Save this summary as the PRIOR CONTEXT bridge for the next related session.

If the session produced artifacts (documents, code, frameworks), ensure they've been delivered as files. If insights emerged that should be searchable later, note them for ingestion into the KLEM/OS knowledge base when that pipeline is operational.

6.4 Session Hygiene

Fresh conversations for fresh topics. Don't chain unrelated tasks into a single mega-session. Context windows are large but not infinite, and accumulated history from unrelated tasks dilutes retrieval relevance and wastes tokens on irrelevant context.

Same conversation for iterative refinement. If you're building a deliverable across multiple turns (whitepaper, architecture, plan), stay in the same session so prior context is available. Use the compaction system when the conversation gets long — it preserves key information while freeing context space.

Quarterly preamble review. As your skills, certifications, projects, and preferences evolve, update the preamble. The audit framework (KLEM/OS v2, Section 5) includes a prompt framework review in the quarterly checklist. A preamble that says "MS-AI candidate" should say "MS-AI, CISSP" after you pass the cert.

                                  If staying: "Parking [current

The Principle Behind All of This

Tell the LLM who you are (once), what you want (specifically), what angles to consider (at minimum), and what bounds apply. Include instructions for how to handle the edges. Then get out of the way and let it synthesize. Iterate surgically. Capture what matters. Start fresh when the context is spent.
